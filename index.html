Masonboro Island · Coastal Field Guide

The mainland. The crossing. The island.

Masonboro Island is eight miles of undeveloped barrier island between Wrightsville Beach and Carolina Beach — no roads, no bridges, boat access only. A state reserve, a wildlife sanctuary, and a trip that rewards planning. This is the field guide.

Higher-than-normal water, faster currents, less exposed shoreline. Marsh paths may be flooded. Plan extra time and do not push a crossing at peak tide.

Where to Launch

Trails End
Public boat ramp at the south end of Masonboro Loop Rd. Kayak and small-boat friendly. Limited parking.
Wrightsville Beach
Small craft launch at Banks Channel. Good for paddlecraft and shorter crossings to the north end of the island.
Carolina Beach
Inlet-side ramps for boats. Longer run to Masonboro but quick water once you're in.
Charter
Several local shuttles and charters drop visitors on the island. Worth it for overnight or family trips.

What to Bring

  • Water — more than you think. There's none on the island.
  • Sun protection: hat, sunscreen, long sleeves for exposure
  • Insect repellent (late spring through fall especially)
  • Shoes that can handle wet sand, shell, and marsh mud
  • Dry bag for phone, keys, wallet
  • PFD for anyone in a paddlecraft (required)
  • Trash bag — you pack out what you bring in
  • Navigation: offline map or compass; phone signal is spotty
  • Tide chart or a plan around the window below

Overnight Only

Primitive camping is allowed for up to 2 consecutive nights. Add: tent, sleeping pad + bag, headlamp, stove or fire pan + firewood (ground fires are permitted — see rules), food in sealed containers, extra water for the night, layers for temperature swings. Personal property must be off the Reserve within 48 hours.

Reserve Rules at a Glance

  • Primitive camping permitted — max 2 consecutive nights
  • Fires allowed on open sand 10+ ft from vegetation; fire pan with 3″+ sides recommended
  • No motor vehicles on the island
  • Dogs permitted; control required at all times
  • Sea turtle nests are marked and federally protected — avoid
  • Stay off vegetated dunes; use existing trails
  • Pack out all trash; personal property must leave within 48 hours
  • Bird nesting areas may be seasonally closed

Source. Rules summarized from NC Department of Environmental Quality · Coastal Reserve. See camping & campfire rules and Subchapter 7O reserve rules for the authoritative text. Check before you go — regulations change.

02 · The Crossing

The waterway.

The crossing is the trip. The Intracoastal Waterway runs the length of the island's back side — busy, tidal, shallow in places, with a real channel to respect.

kayaking

Kayak / SUP

Most common way across. Plan for 30–60 min from Trails End depending on tide and current.

Launch with tide · PFD required

sailing

Small Boat

Faster, more gear capacity, easier return. Anchor in the lee of the island off a sandbar.

Watch channel · shallow at low

directions_boat

Charter / Shuttle

Drop-off service runs from several local operators. Best for families, overnights, or if you don't have a boat.

Pre-book · weather-dependent

warning Do Not Swim Across

The ICW Is Not a Swim Crossing

The Intracoastal Waterway is a federal shipping channel with fast tidal currents, cold water that can cause hypothermia, low-visibility chop, and constant boat traffic that cannot see or stop for a swimmer. People die attempting this every year on the NC coast.

Use a kayak, board, or boat — always with a PFD.

What to Watch For

Current
Tidal current in the ICW runs hard on the ebb and flood. Plan to cross on slack tide or with the current behind you, not against it.
Channel Traffic
Powerboats, fishing charters, and big cruisers run the ICW all day. Cross at a right angle, quickly, and make yourself visible.
Depth
Shallow outside the marked channel, especially at low tide. Sandbars shift. A paddle depth check beats a prop strike.
Wind Chop
A 15-knot wind across the ICW makes a paddle crossing miserable. Check the weather card above before committing.
Return Timing
The return is harder than people plan for. Don't get stranded by a tide you didn't account for.

The Rule of the Crossing

You're on a working river. The ICW connects Key West to Maine and everything that moves between. Big boats have right of way over small ones, and small motorized boats have right of way over paddlecraft.

Cross perpendicular, cross quickly, and if you're in a kayak, cross between traffic — not in front of it.

In an Emergency

Dial 911 — it routes to the U.S. Coast Guard for water emergencies. On a VHF radio, hail the Coast Guard on Channel 16. Give your position, the nature of the emergency, and how many people are with you.

warning Safety & Emergency

Read this before you go.

Masonboro Island is wild. There are no lifeguards, no rangers walking the beach, no bathrooms, and often no cell signal. People have been seriously hurt or killed on this stretch of coast. Most of it was avoidable.

emergency If Something Goes Wrong

Life-threatening

911

Dispatcher routes to Coast Guard for water emergencies. Give your exact location — “north end of Masonboro, swam/paddled from Trails End” beats GPS coordinates you can't read off a dying phone.

Marine radio

VHF 16

Standard distress and hailing channel for the U.S. Coast Guard. If you have a VHF radio on your boat, this is where you call for help. Nearby boats also monitor this channel.

Medical

Novant NHRMC

Novant Health New Hanover Regional Medical Center in Wilmington is the nearest Level II trauma hospital. For a real emergency, 911 will route you. Do not try to drive yourself after a serious injury.

checklist

Before You Push Off

  • —Wear a PFD. Every person. The whole trip. NC law requires it on paddlecraft for kids under 13 — wear one anyway.
  • —Tell someone your float plan: where you're launching, where you're going, who's with you, when you expect to be back.
  • —Check the tide and weather within an hour of launch. Both change fast.
  • —Phone in a dry bag, fully charged. Don't rely on cell signal — it drops out on the island.
  • —Do not go alone. If something goes wrong, a second person is the difference between a scare and a search.
  • —Know your skill level. The ICW eats beginner paddlers who misjudge the current. Start with shorter, calmer trips first.
block

When NOT to Go

  • —Lightning anywhere nearby. Flat water + open sky + fiberglass paddle = worst place to be.
  • —Winds over 15 mph if you're paddling. The ICW gets choppy fast.
  • —Fog, heavy rain, or visibility under a quarter mile.
  • —Against an active storm or hurricane advisory. Check the weather card above.
  • —After drinking or using anything that affects judgment, balance, or reaction time.
  • —If you can't confidently swim 100 yards in open water without stopping.
  • —First trip and going alone. Bring someone who's done it. Or don't go yet.

Conditions change. Local knowledge beats any website — check with a local outfitter, a ranger at the NC Coastal Reserve, or the U.S. Coast Guard before committing to a trip that's new to you. 03 · On the Island

Once you're across.

Eight miles of undeveloped barrier island. Open ocean on one side, salt marsh and ICW on the other. What to do, what to watch for, and how to leave it how you found it.

pets

Wildlife

Shorebirds
Plovers, terns, oystercatchers, pelicans. Nesting areas are marked and may be closed seasonally. Give them distance.
Sea Turtles
Loggerheads nest May–August. Nests are fenced and staked — these are federally protected under the Endangered Species Act. Never approach a nest, never touch a turtle or egg, never shine white light near nests at night (disorients hatchlings). Use red-filtered light only. Report disturbance to NC Wildlife Resources Commission.
Ghost Crabs
Active at night and at dusk. Small holes in the wrack line are burrows. Fun to watch with a red light; leave them be.
Alligators
Give wide distance — at least 60 feet. Occasional in brackish ponds on the island interior. Never feed one (illegal in NC and makes them dangerous to the next person). Do not swim in inland ponds. If one hisses or opens its mouth at you, it's warning you — back away slowly.
Sharks (surf zone)
Bull sharks and blacktips are present in NC waters. Incidents are rare. Don't swim at dawn/dusk, don't swim alone, don't swim near bait schools or fishing piers, avoid shiny jewelry.
Dolphins
Common in the ICW and sometimes the surf zone. A good morning trip reward.
explore

What to Do

Beachcombing
Shelling is excellent after storms. Whelk, scotch bonnet, sand dollars. Take a few; leave live animals.
Surfing
Uncrowded breaks along the 8 miles of Atlantic shore. Best on south or east swell. No lifeguards anywhere on Masonboro — surf with a buddy, know the rip-current forecast, and don't paddle out beyond your skill level.
Walking
The whole island is walkable at low tide. Plan a route that doesn't maroon you at the far end — the tide comes in faster than people expect, and the interior has soft sand, biting insects, and brackish ponds. Bring more water than you think.
Camping
Permitted in designated areas. Primitive — pack everything in and out. See reserve rules above.
Night Sky
One of the darker coastal stretches in the region. Rocket launches and meteor showers are genuinely worth the night trip. A night trip is a different risk profile: visibility is poor, water is colder, boat traffic is still active, and rescue response takes longer. Go with someone experienced, bring working headlamps for each person, and file a detailed float plan.
Leave It Right

Masonboro works because people treat it right.

There's no staff, no ranger walking the beach, no bathrooms to ask about. The island works because visitors don't dump trash, don't cut through dunes, don't mess with nests, and don't leave anything behind.

Pack it out. Every bottle, every wrapper, every trace. If you find something that shouldn't be there, take it too. The next family on this sandbar is counting on you.

Tonight's Sky

What's up there .

Masonboro faces open ocean with no city glow to the east. The dark-sky viewing is the other reason to go — and when there's a rocket launch from Cape Canaveral or a meteor shower peaking, it's the reason.
